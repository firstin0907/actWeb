What is Affect Control Theory and EPA Profile?

A brief introduction to EPA scores and ACT
Affect Control Theory (ACT) is a theory in social psychology used to understand people’s emotions toward specific objects and their social interactions. In this theory, the emotions that people hold toward a given identity, behavior, or modifier are measured along the following three dimensions.
Dimension Description
Evaluation Emotions ranging from positive (+) to negative (−)
Potency Emotions ranging from strong (+) to weak (−)
Activity Emotions ranging from active (+) to passive (−)
For example, in Chinese cultural contexts, the word “mother” tends to have a very high Evaluation score (very positive), a high Potency score (powerful), and a high Activity score (active). The scale of a word evaluated along these three dimensions is called its EPA profile.

As people engage in social life, they form expectations based on these EPA profiles and regulate their emotions to maintain consistency between expectations and actual experiences. That is, if an actor behaves in a way that does not match expectations associated with that actor, people tend to reinterpret the meaning of the behavior to preserve consistency. For example, if a “criminal” (low Evaluation score) performs a “kind” action (high Evaluation score), people may suspect the intent behind the action or reinterpret it negatively because it does not align with expectations.

EPA profiles are calculated from survey data (semantic differential scales) collected across various cultures, and thus they reflect the norms of each culture. A behavior that is evaluated positively in one culture may be evaluated negatively in another. By comparing the EPA profiles assigned to words across cultures, researchers can analyze differences in social expectations among different cultural contexts.

Note this...

As mentioned above, the most of the EPA mean values are calculated as the averages of male and female survey responses within each culture sample. This means that the mean EPA values are just asymptotic averages, not weighted by the actual population. Although studies recruit approximately equal numbers of men and women, and men and women's ratings do not differ substantially on most terms, there may still need for caution.
